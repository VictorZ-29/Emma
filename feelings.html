Leçon 07

Feelings & how you’re doing

Past small talk, friendship happens in the honest answers: I’m shattered, I’m thrilled, I’ve had it with this week. This lesson is the vocabulary of actually saying how you are — and comforting someone who isn’t doing great.

The one idea

French splits feelings between two verbs. States use être — je suis crevée, contente, stressée. But a whole family of feelings are things you have: j’ai faim, j’ai hâte, j’ai la flemme, j’en ai marre. Learn which verb owns which feeling and you’re most of the way there.

Listen first: one of those weeks

You’re running on empty and Max calls. Shadow your lines — being tired in French is a skill you will definitely need.

States: je suis…

All of these agree with you — as a woman you add the -e (you’ll mostly only see it in writing).

FrenchMeans
Je suis crevée.I’m knackered (THE everyday word)
Je suis trop contente !I’m so happy!
Je suis un peu stressée.I’m a bit stressed
Je suis énervée.I’m wound up / annoyed
Je suis déçue.I’m disappointed
Je suis en pleine forme.I’m on great form

Feelings you have: j’ai…

FrenchMeans
J’ai faim. / J’ai soif.I’m hungry / thirsty
J’ai sommeil.I’m sleepy
J’ai trop hâte !I can’t wait!
J’ai la flemme.I can’t be bothered
J’en ai marre.I’m fed up (with it)
J’ai peur de l’avion.I’m scared of flying

Don’t translate “I am hungry”

English is the odd one out here — French (like German: ich habe Hunger) has hunger, thirst, fear and sleepiness. « Je suis faim » doesn’t exist. If the feeling is on this list, it takes avoir.

Bad days — saying it, and hearing it

When someone asks « ça va ? » and it isn’t, here’s the honest register:

Ça va pas fort, aujourd’hui.

“Not great today, honestly.” The standard way to admit it without drama.

J’en peux plus, cette semaine.

“I’m done in this week.” For overload of any kind.

J’ai pas trop le moral.

“I’m feeling a bit low.” Le moral = your spirits.

And when it’s the other person having the rough week, this is your comfort kit:

SayWhen
Ah mince…“oh no…” — first response
Qu’est-ce qui se passe ?“what’s going on?”
Je comprends.“I get it”
Ça va aller.“it’ll be OK”
Courage !“hang in there!”
Repose-toi bien.“rest up properly”

What people actually say

The casual intensifier is trop, not très: je suis trop contente, j’ai trop hâte, c’est trop bon. Très is perfectly correct — trop is what friends sound like. And good things “do you good”: « ça fait du bien » works after a nap, a swim, a rant or a holiday.

Your turn

Tell a friend you’re shattered because you slept terribly.

A friend answers « ça va pas fort ». Respond so they feel heard.

Your best friend is visiting next weekend. Say you can’t wait and you’re so happy.

Honestly, you can’t be bothered to go out — suggest a quiet one instead.

Quick check

Pain also uses avoir: j’ai mal à la tête (headache), j’ai mal au dos (back), j’ai mal partout (everything hurts — post-hike essential). Pattern: avoir mal à + the body part.

Crever literally means to burst or puncture — a tyre is crevé, and so are you after a long week. Same image as English “deflated”, but stronger and more common. (You may also hear « je suis morte » — “I’m dead” — which works exactly like in English.)

Râler = to grumble, and it’s a national sport, done with love. « Il râle tout le temps, mais il est adorable » — he grumbles constantly, but he’s lovely. Knowing this verb explains roughly 30% of French café conversation.
