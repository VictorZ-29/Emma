How French really sounds

You can already read French and pronounce it fine. The hard part is the other direction — understanding it when someone fires it back at full speed. This lesson is about your ear: why fast French sounds like one long blur, and how to turn it back into words (and sound more natural yourself).

The one idea

Spoken French isn’t read word by word — it’s streamed. Words link together, the rhythm stays flat, and whole sounds get swallowed. That’s not sloppiness; it’s the rules. Learn the patterns and the blur snaps back into words.

Listen to real speed first

Play the whole thing once — it’ll sound fast. Then go line by line and say each one back (shadowing). After that, the notes show you exactly what made it hard to catch.

1 · Words run together (liaison)

This is the number-one reason fast French is hard to chop into words. A normally-silent final consonant wakes up and links onto the next word when it starts with a vowel — so two words become one sound.

Why you can’t find the gaps

Liaison glues the end of one word to the start of the next, so the spaces you see on the page aren’t there in the sound. Your ear has to learn to put them back — that’s most of the “they speak so fast” feeling.

2 · The rhythm is flat — the lift is at the end

English punches one syllable and rushes the rest (im-POR-tant). French does the opposite: every syllable gets about the same length, with only a gentle rise on the last syllable of the group. Even it out and you instantly sound less English.

important

“im-por-tant” — three even beats, the tiniest lift on -tant. No heavy stress in the middle.

la possibilité

Five even syllables, rising only at the very end.

On va au restaurant.

The whole phrase stays level and lifts on the final syllable — -rant.

The quickest accent fix

Stop stressing words the English way. Keep every syllable even and let only the end of the phrase rise. Flat + lift-at-the-end = French.

4 · The “ne” disappears

You learned negation as ne … pas. In casual speech the ne is simply dropped — you’ll see it in writing, but rarely hear it. Train your ear to not wait for it.

je sais pas

Spoken form of je ne sais pas — “I don’t know”.

c’est pas grave

ce n’est pas grave — “it’s no big deal”.

j’ai pas faim

je n’ai pas faim — “I’m not hungry”.

For the ear, and for speaking

Drop the ne when you’re chatting and you’ll sound natural. But when you write, put it back: je ne sais pas, ce n’est pas grave.

5 · Asking with your voice

In speech you mostly turn a sentence into a question just by lifting your tone at the end — not by flipping the words around. Same words, different melody:

Tu viens.

A statement — the voice stays level and settles down. “You’re coming.”

Tu viens ?

Same two words, voice lifts at the end → a question. “Are you coming?”

Way to askExampleRegister
Rising toneTu viens ?most common in speech
est-ce queEst-ce que tu viens ?neutral, always safe
InversionViens-tu ?formal / written — stiff in chat

What to actually do

Default to rising tone or est-ce que. Keep inversion (viens-tu ?) for writing — out loud it sounds like a textbook.

Sounds you already own

Your German ear gives you several of the “hard” French sounds for free: French u (tu, rue) is the German ü; French ou is the German u; French ch (chat, chocolat) is the German sch; and the throaty French R is close to the ch in Bach. Don’t learn those from scratch — you’ve got them.

Your turn

Read each cue and say it out loud the natural, spoken way — then reveal a model answer and compare.

Tell a friend, casually, that there’s a problem.

Say “I don’t know” the way you’d say it to a mate (drop the ne).

Ask “are you coming?” without using inversion.

Tell a friend you’re exhausted (you’re speaking about yourself).

Quick check

You’ll only ever hear these crushed-down versions. Can you decode them?

When -ent is the “they” verb ending, you don’t pronounce it at all: ils parlent sounds exactly like il parle. So you often can’t hear “he” vs “they” — context tells you which. (Elsewhere, as in souvent, -ent is a normal nasal vowel.)

It’s made at the back of the throat, not with the tip of the tongue — close to the sound of gently clearing your throat, or the German/Swiss ch in Bach. Try Paris , rouge , merci . Don’t roll it like Spanish or Italian.

A few links are actually forbidden. The clean one to remember: never link after et (“and”). et alors is “é — alors”, never “é-t-alors”. So when you hear no link after a word, that’s information too.
