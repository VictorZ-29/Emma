Sounds & Pronunciation

Good news first: French spelling is far more consistent than English. Learn a handful of patterns and you can read almost any word aloud correctly — even ones you’ve never seen.

📌

The big idea

English makes you memorise “though, through, tough, cough”. French doesn’t do that to you. Once a letter combination has a sound, it keeps it. So this lesson is mostly a list of patterns — not a list of exceptions.

German shortcut

You already own two of the hardest sounds. French u (tu, rue) is the German ü — same rounded “ee”. And French ou is just the German u. Also ch in French = the German sch (chat, chocolat). Three sounds you don’t have to learn from scratch.

Nasal vowels — the genuinely new sound

This one is new (German and English don’t really have it). When a vowel is followed by n or m, you don’t pronounce the n/m — instead the air goes through your nose and colours the vowel.

⚠️

Don’t say the n

In vin you never hear a hard “n” — the n just tells your nose to get involved. Saying “vinn” is the #1 giveaway of an English accent.

Silent endings

French loves to write letters it doesn’t say — especially final consonants. petit, grand, beaucoup all end in a silent letter.

💡

The “CaReFuL” trick

Final consonants are usually silent — except c, r, f, l, which are usually pronounced (the consonants in the word CaReFuL). So you do hear the end of avec, bonjour, chef, animal. Handy exception: most -er verbs and words sound like “-ay” (parler → /e/).

What the accents do

Accents aren’t decoration — they change a sound or tell two words apart.

é

acute — closes the e to “ay”: été (summer)

è · ê

grave / circumflex — opens the e to “eh”: père (father), fête (party)

ç

cédille — keeps c soft (an “s” sound) before a, o, u: ça, garçon

A normally-silent final consonant wakes up and links to the next word when that word starts with a vowel. So vous avez becomes “vou-z-avez”, and les amis becomes “lay-z-amis”. It’s why spoken French flows together rather than landing word by word.

It’s made at the back of the throat, not with the tip of the tongue — close to the sound you make clearing your throat gently, or the German/Swiss “ch” in Bach. Try Paris, rouge, merci. Don’t roll it like Spanish or Italian.

When -ent is the “they” verb ending, it’s completely silent: ils parlent sounds exactly like il parle. (Elsewhere, as in souvent, -ent is a normal nasal vowel.)
