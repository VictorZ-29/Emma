Leçon 12

Polite & hypothetical chunks

Most of this site runs in the friends-register. This last lesson is the other gear: asking strangers for things, favours from neighbours, requests that land softly. The trick is one tense — the conditional — plus three frozen il faut que chunks.

The one idea

French politeness isn’t vocabulary, it’s mood: swap “can” for “could” and the whole sentence bows slightly. Je peux → je pourrais. Vous pouvez → vous pourriez. Il faut → il faudrait. Learn the handful of forms below and you can be graceful anywhere.

Listen first: a favour from the neighbour

All vous, all conditional, and notice — no dropped ne here. This is the register for people you don’t know well yet.

The magic forms

Not a conjugation table — a shortlist. These eight cover almost everything:

ChunkMeans
Je voudrais…I’d like… (shops, cafés, everywhere)
Je pourrais… ?could I…?
Tu pourrais… ?could you…? (friends)
Vous pourriez… ?could you…? (polite)
On pourrait…we could… (suggestions)
Il faudrait…we’d need to / one ought to
J’aimerais…I’d love to…
Ça serait super.that would be great

🇩🇪 Same softener, same slots

This is exactly the German polite machinery: je voudrais = ich möchte, vous pourriez… ? = könnten Sie…?, ça serait… = das wäre…. Wherever your German would go subjunctive-polite, your French goes conditional. The instinct transfers one-to-one.

Asking for things, softly

Excusez-moi de vous déranger…

“Sorry to disturb you…” — the standard opener for any unsolicited ask. Learn it whole.

Ça vous dérangerait de fermer la fenêtre ?

“Would you mind closing the window?” — the gentlest request shape in the language.

Je peux te demander un truc ?

“Can I ask you something?” — the friends-register version. Politeness scales down too.

Ça te dérange si j’ouvre ?

“Do you mind if I open (the window)?” — asking permission, casual version.

The three il faut que chunks

Yes, technically this is the subjunctive. No, you are not learning the subjunctive — you’re learning three fixed phrases that natives fire off daily:

Il faut que j’y aille.

“I’ve got to go.” THE exit line — it ends half the conversations in France. Casual: « faut que j’y aille ! »

Il faut que je te raconte !

“I have GOT to tell you (what happened)!” — the gossip opener. Instant intimacy.

Il faut qu’on se voie plus souvent.

“We must see each other more often.” The warm goodbye between friends.

What people actually say

Nobody leaves by announcing « je pars » — it sounds abrupt. The choreography is: « bon, il faut que j’y aille » + a reason-ish gesture + « on se dit à bientôt ? ». Departure takes three sentences minimum; that’s the culture, and now you have the script.

Your turn

Ask a stranger — politely — if they could take a photo of you.

The evening’s lovely but it’s late. Start your exit, the French way.

Ask a friend if they could help you move a sofa on Saturday — could + would-be-great.

Ask the café owner if they’d mind watching your bag for two minutes.

Quick check

You can be exquisitely polite in tu (« tu pourrais…, ça te dérangerait… ») and brusque in vous. The pronoun sets distance; the conditional sets gentleness. Friends deserve “could you” too.

The same forms carry hypotheticals: « ce serait bien d’avoir une terrasse » (it’d be nice to have a terrace), « j’aimerais trop vivre à Paris » (I’d so love to live in Paris). Suggest with « et si on… ? » + imparfait: « et si on partait en week-end ? »

For conversation: barely. Keep collecting il faut que + phrase as ready-made chunks (il faut que je file — I must dash; il faut que tu voies ça — you have to see this). If you ever get it “wrong”, absolutely everyone will still understand — natives fumble it too.
