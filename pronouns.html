Leçon 10

Pronouns so you don’t repeat yourself

The difference between “Have you seen the film? — Yes, I have seen the film” and “— Yes, I’ve seen it” is the difference between sounding laboured and sounding fluent. This is the lesson that shrinks your sentences.

The one idea

Four little words replace almost every noun: le/la/les (it, them), lui/leur (to him/her, to them), y (there), en (of it, some). One rule of placement: they queue up before the verb — je le vois, never je vois le.

Listen first: the film you have to see

Eight lines, not a single repeated noun. Shadow it, then look at how much work l’, y, en and te are doing.

le, la, les — it and them

Question-and-answer pairs are the fastest way to feel it. Say both halves out loud:

Tu regardes la série ? — Oui, je la regarde.

“Are you watching the series?” — “Yes, I’m watching it.” La série → la.

T’as les billets ? — Oui, je les ai !

“Got the tickets?” — “Yes, I’ve got them!”

T’as vu Léa ? — Oui, je l’ai vue hier.

“Seen Léa?” — “Yes, I saw her yesterday.” Before a vowel, le/la shrink to l’.

lui, leur — to him, to her, to them

For verbs that go à quelqu’un — parler à, dire à, téléphoner à, offrir à:

T’as parlé à Max ? — Oui, je lui ai parlé.

“Did you talk to Max?” — “Yes, I talked to him.”

Je leur dis quoi, à tes parents ?

“What do I tell them — your parents?” Note the very spoken double mention: pronoun first, noun after.

One word, both genders

Lui means to him and to her — there’s no “to her” word. « Je lui ai téléphoné » could be either; context carries it. Don’t reach for la there.

y and en — you already use them

The intimidating pair, except… you’ve been saying them since lesson one:

Chunk you knowWhat’s inside
On y va !y = there → “let’s go (there)”
Vas-y !“go on / go ahead!”
J’en peux plus.en = of it → “I can’t (take any more) of it”
T’en penses quoi ?en = of it → “what do you think of it?”
Y’en a plus.both at once! → “there’s none left”

The system behind the chunks, in two lines:

Tu vas à la piscine ? — J’y vais tous les mardis.

y replaces a place: “I go there every Tuesday.”

Tu veux du gâteau ? — Oui, j’en veux bien !

en replaces du / de la / des + thing: “I’d love some.” With numbers it sticks around: « j’en ai deux » — I’ve got two (of them).

Moi, toi — pointing at people

Where English stresses a word with its voice (“I think…”), French adds a pronoun:

Moi, je trouve ça super. Et toi ?

“I think it’s great. What about you?” — moi, je… isn’t vain, it’s just how emphasis works.

C’est pour moi ? Merci !

After prepositions it’s the same set: pour moi, avec toi, chez lui, sans elle.

How to actually learn this

Don’t memorise the grammar table — memorise the Q&A pairs above until each answer fires automatically. In conversation you don’t have time to compute “direct or indirect?”; you have time to sound like « oui, je l’ai vu » because you’ve said it fifty times.

Your turn

A friend asks « t’as vu le film ? » — say yes, you’ve seen it, it’s brilliant. No repeating “le film”.

Someone asks if you’ve phoned your parents. Yes — on Sunday. (Phoned to them.)

Asked how often you go to the climbing gym: twice a week — without repeating the place.

You’re offered a third croissant. Decline: you’ve already had two (of them).

Quick check

They stack, people-word first: « tu me le prêtes ? » (you lend it to me?), « je te l’envoie » (I’ll send it to you). Don’t engineer these yet — recognise them, and let the common ones become chunks.

Pronoun and verb hold hands; pas comes after: « je le vois pas » (I can’t see it), « j’y arrive pas » (I can’t manage it — a phrase for gym class and bureaucracy alike).

In « je l’ai vue » (Léa), the participle agrees with the pronoun that came before it. It’s inaudible here and purely a writing detail — but now you know why texts from French friends have those extra -e’s.
