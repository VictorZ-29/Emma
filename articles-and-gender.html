Gender & Articles

Every French noun is either masculine or feminine, and the little word in front — le, la, un, une — tells you which. It looks intimidating; it’s really just one habit to build.

“The” — le · la · l’ · les

French has four words for “the”, depending on the noun’s gender and whether it’s plural.

FormUsed forExample
lemasculine, singularle pain
lafeminine, singularla table
l’before a vowel sound (either gender)l’eau, l’ami
lesplural (either gender)les amis

“A / an” — un · une

un café

un = a/an for masculine nouns.

une pomme

une = a/an for feminine nouns.

💡

The golden rule (do this and gender stops being scary)

Never learn a noun on its own. Don’t memorise “table = table” — memorise “la table”. Learn the article with the word, every single time, and the gender comes for free later. That’s the whole secret, and it’s why every flashcard on this site shows the article.

🇩🇪

German shortcut — and a warning

The concept is one you already have: German nouns are der / die / das, so “nouns have a gender” is nothing new. French just has two genders instead of three (no neuter).

The warning: the genders do not match between the languages. la table is feminine, but der Tisch is masculine. le soleil (sun) is masculine, but die Sonne is feminine. So don’t guess French gender from German — learn it fresh with the article.

They’re tendencies, not laws — but they help you guess when you’re stuck:

Often feminine: -tion (la nation), -té (la liberté), -ette (la baguette), -ure (la voiture).

Often masculine: -age (le voyage), -ment (le moment), -eau (le château), -isme (le tourisme). Watch out: a final -e often looks feminine but isn’t always — le problème, le système are masculine.
