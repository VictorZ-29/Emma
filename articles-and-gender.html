Gender & articles

Not a lesson — a quick reference. You already use le and la fine when you talk; this page is just here for when you want to settle a gender or jog your memory. Skip it until you need it.

💡

The only habit that really matters

Never learn a noun on its own. Don’t store “table = table” — store “la table”. Learn the article with the word, every time, and the gender comes for free later. That’s the whole game, and it’s why every flashcard on this site shows the article.

Guess the gender from the ending

When you’re stuck, the ending of a word is a decent bet. These are tendencies, not laws — but they’ll win you more often than not.

EndingUsuallyExample
-tionfemininela nation
-téfemininela liberté
-ettefemininela baguette
-urefemininela voiture
-agemasculinele voyage
-mentmasculinele moment
-eaumasculinele château
-ismemasculinele tourisme
⚠️

The -e trap

A final -e often looks feminine, but plenty of -e words are masculine — le problème, le système, le programme. When the ending is ambiguous, fall back on the habit above: learn it with its article.

🇩🇪

German helps with the idea — not the genders

The concept is one you already have: German has der / die / das, so “nouns carry a gender” is nothing new. French is even simpler — two genders, no neuter.

The catch: the genders don’t match between the languages. la table is feminine but der Tisch is masculine; le soleil is masculine but die Sonne is feminine. Use German for the instinct, never to guess a French gender.
