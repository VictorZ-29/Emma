Leçon 02

Greetings & Politeness

Enough to walk into a bakery, a café, or a friend’s flat and sound warm and polite from the first word. The trick isn’t vocabulary — it’s knowing which register to use.

Saying hello

Bonjour

The default “hello” for the whole day. Always safe. In a shop, say it the moment you walk in — skipping it reads as rude.

Bonsoir

“Good evening” — swap to this from roughly 6pm.

Salut

Casual “hi” for friends and people your age. Also means “bye”.

Coucou

Very informal and affectionate — for close friends and family (think “heyy”).

“How are you?”

Same idea, three levels of formality. The reply usually loops the question back with Et toi ? / Et vous ? (“And you?”).

Ça va ?

Casual. Literally “it goes?” — answer Ça va, et toi ?

Comment ça va ?

Neutral, friendly. Answer Ça va bien, merci.

Comment allez-vous ?

Formal / polite. Answer Très bien, merci. Et vous ?

📌

tu vs vous — the one thing to get right

tu and vous both mean “you”. Use vous for strangers, shopkeepers, older people, anyone senior, and any group of people. Use tu for friends, family, children, and people your own age once things are relaxed.

When in doubt, use vous — it’s never offensive to be a little too polite, but tu too soon can feel over-familiar.

🇩🇪

German shortcut

This is exactly the German du vs Sie distinction — and the social rules line up almost perfectly. tu = du, vous = Sie. If you have a feel for when to use Sie, you already have a feel for vous.

Introducing yourself

Je m’appelle Emma.

“My name is Emma.” (Literally “I call myself…”.)

Comment tu t’appelles ?

“What’s your name?” (casual). Formal: Comment vous appelez-vous ?

Enchantée !

“Nice to meet you.” Because you’re female, you write it enchantée (extra -e) — a man writes enchanté. They sound identical.

The polite little words

s’il te plaît · s’il vous plaît

“Please” — casual / polite. (Often written STP / SVP in texts.)

merci · merci beaucoup

“Thank you” · “thank you very much.”

de rien

“You’re welcome” (literally “of nothing”).

pardon · excusez-moi

“Sorry” / “excuse me” — to apologise or to get someone’s attention.

Saying goodbye

Au revoir

The standard “goodbye”.

À bientôt · À demain

“See you soon” · “see you tomorrow”.

Bonne journée !

“Have a good day!” — a lovely thing to say as you leave. Evening version: Bonne soirée !

It’s an adjective describing you, so it agrees with your gender — feminine adds an -e. You’ll meet this agreement everywhere (it’s Lesson 07). The spelling changes; the pronunciation doesn’t.

Yes — like the Italian ciao or Hawaiian aloha, salut works on the way in and on the way out. Casual only.
