Leçon 02

Small talk & sounding natural

This one isn’t about vocabulary — it’s about sounding like a friend, not a textbook. By the end you can open a casual chat, answer “how’s it going?” for real, and keep it flowing with the little words French people actually use.

The one idea

Small talk is a ritual — warmth and reacting matter more than perfect sentences. A well-timed « ah ouais ? » does more work than a flawless paragraph. Listen to the dialogue first, then steal the phrases underneath it.

Listen first: two friends meeting

Play the whole thing, then go line by line and say each one back out loud. That copying-aloud trick is called shadowing — it’s how your mouth learns the rhythm.

Saying hi, the casual way

Among friends and people your own age you’ll rarely hear a full bonjour. These are what you’ll actually reach for.

Salut !

The everyday casual “hi” — and it works for “bye” too.

Coucou !

Softer and affectionate — for close friends and family (a bit like “heyy”).

Ça va ?

Doubles as a greeting all on its own — “hey, you alright?” You can lead with it.

What people actually say

The textbook « Comment allez-vous ? » is correct but formal — save it for a job interview or your friend’s grandmother. With anyone relaxed, « Ça va ? » or « Ça roule ? » is the greeting.

Lean on your German

Choosing between tu and vous is the same instinct as du vs Sie — and the social rules line up almost perfectly. Friends, family, people your age → tu / du. Strangers, shopkeepers, anyone older or senior → vous / Sie. If you’d say Sie, say vous.

Read the room

Keep this casual register for friends and peers. With a stranger, a shopkeeper or someone older, open with bonjour and stick to vous and « ça va ? » — not « ça roule ? ».

And the dropped ne (« c’est pas grave ») is for speaking. When you write, put it back: « ce n’est pas grave ».
