Leçon 11

Sounding more French

Not new grammar — small swaps. A handful of habits separate “correct French” from “French that makes people forget you’re foreign”: on instead of nous, c’est everywhere, softeners at the edges, and a pocketful of little idioms.

The one idea

Native-sounding French is mostly default choices, not advanced vocabulary. Every time you pick on over nous and c’est over a fancier verb, you sound one notch more French — at zero extra difficulty.

Listen first: dinner at Max’s

Count the c’ests and the ons. This is what relaxed, warm, ordinary French sounds like.

Swap 1: on is the spoken “we”

On y va ? On prend ma voiture ?

“Shall we go? Take my car?” — everyday “we” is on, and it conjugates like il/elle. One form, no endings to learn: a grammar discount.

Nous, on fait ça autrement.

“We do it differently.” Nous survives out front, for emphasis — then on takes over.

🇩🇪 It’s « man », promoted

You know on already — it’s German man (“one/people”): « on dit que… » = „man sagt…“. French just went one step further and made it the everyday word for we. Keep the German intuition, add the new job.

Swap 2: when in doubt, c’est

For reactions, comments and introductions, c’est is almost always the natural choice:

SituationSay
reacting to anythingC’est génial ! / C’est trop bon !
asking what something isC’est quoi, ça ?
introducing someoneC’est un pote du boulot.
“it’s fine / no need”Laisse, c’est bon.

The one place c’est loses

Bare professions take il/elle est, no article: « il est prof ». But add anything — an article, an adjective — and c’est comes back: « c’est un prof génial ». Rule of thumb: c’est + un/une/mon…, il est + bare job.

Swap 3: let the edges soften

French sentences rarely end cold. These little words carry warmth, doubt, or a shrug — recognise them all, adopt one or two:

SoftenerFlavourIn the wild
…quoi“…you know”C’est compliqué, quoi.
…hein ?“…right? / eh?”Pas mal, hein ?
Bon, ben…“right, well…”Bon, ben on y va ?
Allez !“come on / right then”Allez, à demain !
quand même“still / all the same”C’est cher, quand même.

The idiom pocketful

Five chunks that do a lot of social work. You’ve met some already — here’s the set:

ChunkMeans
Ça marche !deal / works for me

En fait ≠ au fait

Two lookalikes natives use constantly: en fait = “actually” (correcting or revealing — « en fait, j’adore »), au fait = “by the way” (changing subject — « au fait, t’es libre samedi ? »). Mixing them up won’t sink you, but keeping them straight is a quiet flex.

Your turn

Suggest a film tonight using on — not nous.

You taste something amazing at a friend’s place. React, then ask what it is — both with c’est.

Introduce a work friend: who she is, and that she’s really nice.

Everyone’s finally ready to leave. Round them up, the French way.

Quick check

Verlan flips syllables to make slang: femme → meuf, fou → ouf (« c’était un truc de ouf ! » = it was insane), merci → cimer. You don’t need to speak it — but recognising meuf and ouf unlocks a lot of casual chat and most French rap.

It means “still / all the same / even so” — and as an exclamation, polite outrage: « Quand même ! » (“honestly! the cheek!”). « Il aurait pu appeler, quand même » — he could at least have called. One phrase, a whole French attitude.

French shrinks things to make them friendly: un petit café, un petit resto sympa, on se fait une petite rando ? The petit isn’t about size — it makes the suggestion warmer and easier to say yes to. Sprinkle accordingly.
