When you’re lost in a conversation

It will happen — someone talks fast, a word you’ve never met lands in the middle of a sentence, your mind blanks. This lesson is the life-jacket: six phrases that keep you in the conversation instead of smiling and nodding your way out of it.

The one idea

Getting lost isn’t failing — it’s part of conversation, and natives get lost too. What matters is the signal. Say « attends », ask, and carry on. The only real mistake is going silent.

Listen first: keeping up with Hugo

Hugo talks fast. Watch how three little questions keep you in the story — and how normal it all feels.

The two ask-abouts

Keep the pair straight: « ça veut dire quoi, “bondé” ? » when the French word is the mystery, « comment on dit “cheeky” ? » when the English one is. Between them, no unknown word can stop you.

Buying time like a native

French speakers stall out loud, constantly and shamelessly. Do the same and the silence never gets awkward:

Euh… attends…

“Uh… hang on…” — the basic stall.

Comment dire…

“How to put it…” — buys a whole sentence of thinking time.

Attends, je cherche le mot…

“Wait, I’m looking for the word…” — people will happily wait, or offer it.

C’est quoi déjà, le mot… ?

“What’s the word again…?” — that déjà means “remind me”, and natives use it for their own language too.

Checking you got it

Si je comprends bien, on se voit vendredi ?

“So if I’ve understood right, we’re meeting Friday?” Repeat the key fact back — the best insurance there is.

C’est ça ?

“Is that right?” — tag it onto anything. (The happy answer: « c’est ça ! »)

When they switch to English

Some French people will flip to English to “help”. If you want the practice, smile and hold the line: « Je préfère parler français, ça m’entraîne ! » (“I’d rather speak French — it’s good training!”). It’s taken as a compliment, and it works.

Your turn

Your friend is telling a story at rocket speed. Interrupt — kindly — and slow them down.

Someone just called the concert « ouf » and you have no idea what that means. Ask.

You need the French for “to queue” mid-sentence. Ask for it.

You think the plan is 8pm outside the cinema. Check it.

Quick check

All mean “what?”. Hein ? and quoi ? are for friends — direct and completely normal between mates, a bit blunt elsewhere. Pardon ? or « pardon, j’ai pas entendu » is your safe default with anyone you’d vous.

Same image in French: « je l’ai sur le bout de la langue » — it’s on the tip of my tongue. And a mind blank is « un trou » (a hole): « attends, j’ai un trou ». Both make you sound charmingly fluent about not being fluent.

When the word won’t come at all, point at the idea: « le truc pour ouvrir les bouteilles » — the thing for opening bottles. Truc and machin are the official French words for “whatsit”, and natives lean on them daily. Vague is fluent.
