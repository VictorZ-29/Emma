Out in the world (socially)

In France, buying a croissant is a small social exchange with rules and pleasantries — not a transaction. This lesson is the café, bakery and restaurant as conversations: the sacred script, the natural ordering formulas, and the chit-chat that makes people warm to you.

The one idea

Bonjour is a password, not decoration. Every exchange opens with it and closes with a merci, bonne journée. Inside that frame you can make mistakes freely — skip the frame and even perfect French lands badly.

Listen first: breakfast on a terrace

An ordinary order that turns into a small chat — which is exactly how it goes when you’re friendly. Note the vous throughout.

The sacred script

Every shop, café and counter follows the same shape. Have it ready and everything else is improvisation:

Bonjour !

Walk in, make eye contact, say it. After ~6pm it becomes bonsoir.

Je vais prendre une baguette, s’il vous plaît.

“I’ll take…” — the everyday ordering formula.

Merci, bonne journée !

And the close. They’ll wish it back — « bonne journée, au revoir ! »

Insider moves

Tap water is free and normal to ask for: « une carafe d’eau, s’il vous plaît » . Service is included by law (service compris) — locals leave small change for good service, nothing like 20%.

And to catch a waiter’s eye: a raised hand and « s’il vous plaît ! ». Never « garçon ! » — that lives only in old films now.

🇩🇪 The register is the Swiss one

Everyone at a counter is vous — exactly where your Zurich instinct would say Sie. Same greeting culture too: the ritual Grüezi on entering a Swiss shop is precisely the French bonjour. You’ve done all this before, in another language.

Your turn

You walk into a café. Greet and order a coffee and a croissant — the full script.

The menu is long and you can’t decide. Ask the waiter what they recommend.

The meal was lovely and you’re done. Compliment it and ask for the bill.

They ask « sur place ou à emporter ? » — you’re staying, and you’d like tap water.

Quick check

Un café = a small espresso. Milky and big = un crème; espresso lengthened with water = un allongé; a dash of milk = une noisette (named for its hazelnut colour). Ordering « un café au lait » marks you as a visitor — « un crème » is what Parisians say.

At the marché, chit-chat is part of the deal: « Elles sont bonnes, vos tomates ? » (“are your tomatoes good?”) is a normal opener, and they’ll often hand you something to taste — « goûtez ! ». Say « je vais en prendre un kilo » and you’re practically a regular.

L’entrée = the starter (not the main!). Le menu = the fixed-price set; the card you order from is la carte. And un pichet is the honest jug of house wine — often the best-value thing on the table.
